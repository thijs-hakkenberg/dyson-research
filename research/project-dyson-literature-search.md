Paper #1 — Plachta & Kittel, “An Updated Zero Boil-Off Cryogenic Propellant Storage

Citation: Plachta, D.W. & Kittel, P. “An Updated Zero Boil-Off Cryogenic Propellant
Storage Analysis Applied to Upper Stages or Depots in a LEO Environment.” NASA/TM-

E1. Microgravity Electrolysis Breakthrough
Paper #48 — Akay, Romero-Calvo et al., “Magnetically Induced Convection Enhances
Water Electrolysis in Microgravity”
Citation: Akay, Ö., Monfort-Castillo, M., St Francis, T., Becker, J., Saravanabavan, S.,
Romero-Calvo, Á. & Brinkert, K. “Magnetically induced convection enhances water
electrolysis in microgravity.” Nature Chemistry, 17, 1673–1679, 2025.
URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41557-025-01890-0 | PMC:
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12580345/
Annotation: Demonstrates that a commercial neodymium magnet can achieve up to
240% current density improvement in microgravity water electrolysis by exploiting
magnetic polarization and magnetohydrodynamic forces. Achieves passive gas-liquid
phase separation without mechanical centrifuges. Two architectures demonstrated: PEM
electrolyzer and MHD drive, both producing O₂/H₂ at near-terrestrial efficiency in drop-
tower microgravity.
RQs: rq-0-33
Paper #56 — Romero-Calvo et al., “Magnetic phase separation in microgravity”
Citation: Romero-Calvo, Á., Akay, Ö., Schaub, H. & Brinkert, K. “Magnetic phase
separation in microgravity.” npj Microgravity, 8, 1–10, 2022.
URL: DOI: 10.1038/s41526-022-00212-9
Annotation: Drop tower validation of magnetic phase separation concept — the
precursor work to the Nature Chemistry 2025 breakthrough. Demonstrates that
permanent magnets can control gas-liquid interfaces in microgravity via diamagnetic
force on water. Establishes the fundamental physics exploited in the later
electrochemical applications.
