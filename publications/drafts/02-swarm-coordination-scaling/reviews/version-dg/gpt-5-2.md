## 1. Significance & Novelty  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
The manuscript targets an important and under-served problem: *parametric sizing* of coordination communications for very large autonomous swarms, with explicit byte accounting and TDMA airtime feasibility. The two-test framework (byte budget + TDMA schedulability) and the “rate ladder” from info-rate to PHY-rate are useful abstractions for early-phase design. The emphasis on per-cluster sizing (rather than end-to-end network routing) is appropriate for a first-order design paper.

Novelty is strongest in (i) the explicit decomposition of feasibility into Test A/Test B with clear parameter dependencies, (ii) the practitioner-oriented \(\gamma(R_{\text{PHY}})\) time-domain formulation tied to CCSDS Prox-1 framing assumptions, and (iii) the explicit coupling discussion between retransmissions and TDMA margin. Novelty is weaker where the paper re-derives known TDMA efficiency expressions and where DES is used primarily to reconfirm deterministic accounting.

---

## 2. Methodological Soundness  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The analytical accounting is generally consistent and the separation between message-layer accounting and slot-layer airtime is conceptually sound. The slot budget in Model C is well specified (payload, framing, FEC, guard, acquisition). The Monte Carlo setup is adequate for estimating means and selected tails under the assumed workload models.

However, several methodological choices limit the strength of the claims for a top-tier journal: (1) the DES is cycle-aggregated and largely deterministic in the service model; it cannot validate MAC/PHY dynamics, and the authors correctly admit this—yet some conclusions (e.g., “35 kbps recommended”) depend strongly on unvalidated acquisition/guard assumptions and on a highly stylized GE coherence model; (2) the GE model is treated as a “what-if tool,” but the mapping from physical phenomena to \((p_{BG}, p_{GB}, p_B)\) remains speculative; (3) the ARQ provisioning analysis mixes expected-value and high-quantile arguments in a way that needs tightening (details in Major Issues).

---

## 3. Validity & Logic  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
Internal logic is mostly coherent, and Version DG appears to have improved clarity on several prior pitfalls:  
- The campaign duty factor \(d\) is now explicitly framed as episodic workload gating, and the \(\eta_S\approx 46\%\) case is contextualized as a continuous-duty upper bound occurring \(<1\%\) of time.  
- The “\(\gamma\) unification” around \(\approx 0.76\) (CCSDS-grounded) is much more consistently applied, with Model S clearly labeled as non-design/upper-bound.

That said, there remain logical tensions: the paper sometimes implies a “three-layer feasibility” (byte budget, MAC efficiency, TDMA airtime) while also insisting \(\gamma\) is not a separate test. The manuscript tries to resolve this with the boxed note, but the narrative still risks double-counting or confusing readers about what is being tested versus converted. Additionally, some “recommendation” statements read stronger than the stated evidence tier supports.

## 4. Clarity & Structure  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
Overall organization is strong: notation table, explicit test definitions, rate ladder table, and an algorithmic sizing procedure are all helpful. The explicit disclaimer that there is no external validation is unusually clear (a strength). The separation of Model C (design) vs Model S (bound) is much improved and mostly consistent.

Opportunities remain: (i) the paper is long and occasionally repetitive (multiple re-statements that \(\gamma\) is not a separate test); (ii) some tables mix “illustrative” and “design-relevant” results in a way that invites mis-citation (e.g., Table IV-D / Table 9); (iii) several numerical claims would benefit from a single consolidated “assumption ledger” (slot timing, ACK handling, half-duplex partition rule, and whether coordinator also sends status).

## 6. Scope & Referencing  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The cited literature is broadly relevant (CCSDS, DVB-RCS2, AoI survey, Raft, DTN, constellation networking). However, for IEEE TAES / Advances in Space Research, the paper would benefit from deeper anchoring in:  
- Satellite MAC/TDMA demand assignment and return-link scheduling literature beyond DVB-RCS2 (including more recent NGSO/ISL MAC work).  
- Spacecraft crosslink modem acquisition/turnaround timing references (vendor app notes, published mission modem performance, or CCSDS practice reports) to better justify \(T_{\text{acq}}\) and turnaround/jitter assumptions.  
- Work on bursty traffic models and buffer sizing for MMPP/ON-OFF sources with deterministic service (queueing theory references are present, but the tail/buffer conclusions are currently positioned as too design-actionable given the limited model).

# Major Issues

1. **Independent validation remains thin; Section IV-J “packet-level validation” is not truly independent**  
   - **Why it matters:** The key design recommendation (30 kbps minimum, 35 kbps recommended) is highly sensitive to \(\gamma\), \(T_{\text{acq}}\), and \(T_{\text{guard}}\). The “standards-based” derivation is valuable, but it is not independent validation of real modem behavior. Currently, Tier-2 is largely “simulation consistent with assumed timing,” not “validated against a distinct source of truth.”  
   - **Remedy:** Strengthen Section IV-J by (a) explicitly distinguishing *parameter anchoring* vs *validation* in the section title and narrative (you do this partially), and (b) adding at least one additional, external anchoring point: e.g., published Prox-1 implementation timing, a vendor burst-mode acquisition statistic, or a small hardware-in-the-loop microbenchmark if feasible. If no external data can be added, the paper should soften “recommended PHY” language and present it as “design point under assumed Prox-1-class burst acquisition timing,” emphasizing sensitivity bands.

2. **ARQ feasibility analysis mixes coherence assumptions and provisioning logic; needs a tighter, consistent queueing/quantile treatment**  
   - **Why it matters:** The argument “30 kbps is marginally infeasible for ARQ under GE; 35 kbps is feasible” hinges on (i) coherence regime (\(\tau_c \ge T_c\) vs \(\tau_c \ll T_c\)), and (ii) distribution of simultaneous failures requiring retransmission within the residual margin. The paper uses \(E[\text{failed}]\) and then a “P95 binomial” approximation, but the GE model induces correlation across nodes only implicitly (shared state?) and across time explicitly; also, the “same channel state for all retransmissions” assumption is per-link, but the analysis treats failures as independent across nodes in a cycle.  
   - **Remedy:**  
     - Specify whether GE is per-node independent, per-link independent, or *cluster-common* (shared fading state). This is critical: cluster-common bad states produce far heavier tails than binomial.  
     - Replace the mixed mean/P95 reasoning with a consistent statement: either (a) assume independent per-node losses and compute the distribution of failures and required slots, or (b) assume a common-state model and compute the induced distribution (mixture distribution) for number of failures.  
     - Report the resulting miss probability at 30 and 35 kbps under each assumption. This can be done analytically (mixture-of-binomials) and confirmed in slot-sim.

3. **The “three-layer feasibility framework” is still conceptually ambiguous in presentation**  
   - **Why it matters:** You correctly state \(\gamma\) is a parameter inside Test B and not a separate test, but other parts (and the user-facing “rate ladder”) can be read as three checks: bytes → “MAC efficiency” → airtime. Practitioners may misapply the method (double converting by \(1/\gamma\), or treating \(C_{\text{TDMA}}\) as a separate capacity constraint).  
   - **Remedy:** Reframe the narrative as:  
     - Test A: information-byte budget.  
     - Test B: airtime budget, with a *single* slot-time equation that already includes FEC/framing/guard/acq (i.e., compute \(T_{\text{slot}}\) directly; \(\gamma\) is optional as a reporting convenience).  
     - Move “rate ladder” to an explanatory sidebar and explicitly state it is algebraic decomposition of Test B, not additional constraints. Consider renaming “Step 2 Slot overhead (1/γ)” to “Slot-time expansion factor (derived from slot model).”

4. **Campaign duty factor \(d\) improves realism, but workload realism still depends heavily on \(p_{\text{cmd}}=1\) during ‘on’ campaigns and on command size assumptions**  
   - **Why it matters:** The main overhead conclusions (“routine 5–10%,” “46% stress bound”) depend on a specific command model: 512 B commands, potentially every cycle during active periods. In many real operations, command traffic is not per-cycle even during campaigns; it is bursty but not necessarily periodic at 0.1 Hz. Conversely, some autonomy schemes might generate more frequent small deltas.  
   - **Remedy:** Add a short sensitivity subsection (or table) showing \(\eta\) and egress airtime versus (i) command size (already partly done), (ii) command rate within campaign (\(p_{\text{cmd}}\) < 1), and (iii) mixture of small/large commands. Provide a recommended “default operational envelope” (e.g., \(p_{\text{cmd}}\in[0.05,0.2]\) during campaigns) and show how that changes the need for 35 kbps.

5. **DES verification value: currently framed honestly, but still too much manuscript real estate for Tier-1 tautological agreement**  
   - **Why it matters:** IEEE TAES reviewers will question why a DES is needed if it mainly reproduces closed-form means. You note the only incremental value is distributional tails, but then many DES results are still reported and discussed.  
   - **Remedy:** Compress Tier-1 DES mean-agreement discussion to an appendix or a short paragraph; focus the main text on the tail/buffer results and on any emergent behavior not captured in the equations. Alternatively, strengthen DES value by adding at least one scenario where equations are not straightforward (e.g., mixed \(q\), multiple command classes with priorities, or coordinator failure transients affecting backlog).

6. **Packet/slot timing assumptions need a clearer “implementation contract” (ACK placement, turnaround, acquisition per slot)**  
   - **Why it matters:** The feasibility boundary between 30 and 35 kbps is tight; small modeling choices (ACK mini-slot vs embedded ACK, acquisition distribution, turnaround counted once vs twice) can move the boundary by several kbps. While you discuss this, the assumptions are scattered.  
   - **Remedy:** Provide a single concise “Slot & Superframe Timing Assumption Table” listing: preamble length, framing bits, FEC rate, acquisition model (cold/reacquire/tracking), guard composition, ACK policy, and turnaround handling. Then state: “All Model C design claims use this exact contract.”

# Minor Issues

1. **Table IV-D (ARQ×TDMA coupling) uses Model S only**: even with the footnote, this table is easy to misread as design-relevant. Consider moving it to an appendix or renaming it more explicitly (“Illustration under optimistic timing only”).  
2. **\(\gamma\) rate dependence direction**: the text states \(\gamma\) decreases with \(R_{\text{PHY}}\) because fixed-time overhead dominates at low rates—this is inverted as written. Fixed-time overhead dominates *more* at low rates, so \(\gamma\) should be *lower* at low rates and *higher* at high rates, unless acquisition/guard are fixed-time and payload time shrinks with rate. Please re-check wording and ensure the plotted curve matches the equation. (From your ledger: \(\gamma_{24}=0.761\), \(\gamma_{30}=0.745\), \(\gamma_{35}=0.732\): \(\gamma\) decreases as rate increases, which is non-intuitive; it occurs because \(T_{\text{payload}}\) shrinks while fixed \(T_{\text{acq}}+T_{\text{guard}}\) stays constant. Clarify explicitly.)  
3. **Baseline 20.5%**: confirm whether coordinator also sends a status report and whether that is included/excluded consistently (you use \((k_c-1)\) for ingress, implying coordinator excluded). Make explicit in the baseline definition.  
4. **Equation (Test B ingress with \(\bar{M}_r\))**: \((1+\bar{M}_r)\) suggests per-node average retransmissions; later you treat \(M_r\) as a pool of shared slots. Harmonize notation: either model ARQ as shared pool (recommended) or as per-node.  
5. **Fleet reuse**: the reuse factor \(R=7\) derivation is very approximate; consider clearly labeling it as a first-order C/I geometry estimate and avoid implying it is “recommended” without network simulation.  
6. **Some claims are too categorical** (“Undersizing to 24 kbps causes 100% deadline misses under Model C”): this is true for the stated parameters, but add “for \(k_c=100, S=256\)B, cold-start timing” to prevent overgeneralization.  
7. **Reference hygiene**: several “non-archival accessed 2026” references are fine for context but should not be used to support key technical claims (e.g., Kuiper overview). Ensure technical claims rely on archival/standards sources.  
8. **Terminology**: you use “MAC efficiency” and “slot efficiency” somewhat interchangeably. Prefer one term (slot efficiency) since \(\gamma\) is not modeling contention.

## Overall Recommendation  
**Recommendation:** **Major Revision**

The paper has a strong premise and is unusually practitioner-oriented for early-phase swarm coordination sizing. Version DG shows clear improvement in (i) the duty-factor realism framing, (ii) consistent application of the CCSDS-grounded \(\gamma\approx 0.70\)–0.76 values (replacing the earlier optimistic value), and (iii) explicit contextualization of the \(\eta_S\approx 46\%\) case as a continuous-duty upper bound with low operational prevalence. The two-test framework and rate ladder are compelling contributions if presented with unambiguous semantics.

The main blockers for acceptance at a top-tier aerospace systems journal are (1) insufficiently independent validation/anchoring for the timing parameters that drive the 30 vs 35 kbps boundary, and (2) an ARQ/GE analysis that needs a more consistent probabilistic treatment and clearer assumptions about correlation across nodes/links. A revision that tightens the stochastic model, clarifies the feasibility semantics, and either adds external anchoring or softens recommendation strength would substantially improve the manuscript.

## Constructive Suggestions (ordered by impact)

1. **Make the GE/ARQ model unambiguous and quantitatively consistent**: specify correlation structure; use mixture/binomial (or common-state) analytics; report miss probabilities and required retransmission-slot distributions at 30/35 kbps.  
2. **Strengthen (or re-scope) “validation”**: either add an external timing anchor for acquisition/turnaround/slot overhead, or rephrase claims as conditional on measured \(\gamma, T_{\text{acq}}, T_{\text{guard}}\) and present 35 kbps as a robust design point rather than a recommendation.  
3. **Unify Test B around slot-time, not \(\gamma\)**: present \(\gamma\) as a reporting convenience derived from the slot model; adjust the “three-layer” messaging to avoid misapplication.  
4. **Add workload sensitivity within campaigns**: vary \(p_{\text{cmd}}\) and command-size mixtures; show how \(d\) and \(p_{\text{cmd}}\) jointly map to realistic ops timelines.  
5. **Reduce Tier-1 DES emphasis; elevate the non-tautological parts**: tails, failure transients, and any emergent backlog effects.  
6. **Consolidate timing assumptions into a single “contract” table** and ensure every design-relevant table/figure references that contract explicitly.  
7. **Archive reproducibility artifacts** (DOI) and add “one-command” reproduction instructions for key figures/tables.  

If the authors address Major Issues 1–3 robustly, the paper could become a valuable reference for early-phase coordination channel sizing in large swarms.
