## 1. Significance & Novelty  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
The manuscript tackles a practically important and under-served problem: **parametric sizing** of hierarchical coordination traffic and TDMA schedulability for very large swarms/constellations. The paper’s main value is not a new protocol, but a **design-equation workflow** that connects (i) byte-level workload accounting, (ii) slot-efficiency/PHY framing effects, and (iii) feasibility boundaries leading to a concrete recommended PHY rate. That is a meaningful contribution for early-phase system engineering.

Novelty is strongest in: (a) the **closed-form “rate ladder”** and feasibility tests, (b) the explicit **CCSDS-grounded \(\gamma(R_{\text{PHY}})\)** (rate dependence often ignored), and (c) the explicit separation of **byte-budget vs. TDMA airtime** constraints. Novelty is weaker in the GE channel modeling and AoI material (well-known), and in DES results that mostly verify analytic means.

---

## 2. Methodological Soundness  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The analytical accounting is generally careful (clear definitions for baseline vs overhead; explicit slot timing; explicit half-duplex partition). The **two-test framework** is a sound way to prevent unit/abstraction errors that plague cross-layer sizing papers.

However, several methodological choices limit scientific strength:  
* The **DES** is cycle-aggregated and intentionally abstracts MAC into \(\gamma\); that is fine for design equations, but then the DES adds limited new evidence beyond bookkeeping and a particular burst model.  
* The **slot-level simulator** appears to be the real arbiter for schedulability; its assumptions (ACK placement inside guard/jitter budget, acquisition model, ARQ slot reservation policy) need tighter specification to be reproducible and to avoid “hidden degrees of freedom.”  
* The **GE model** is explicitly “what-if,” but then it drives ARQ feasibility conclusions; this is acceptable only if the paper consistently treats those conclusions as conditional, not predictive.

---

## 3. Validity & Logic  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
Internal logic is mostly consistent, and Version CX clearly tries to correct earlier ambiguities (e.g., explicitly labeling Model C as decision-relevant, and contextualizing the 46% stress case). Still, there are a few logical/definition tensions that should be resolved:

* The paper sometimes frames feasibility as “two-layer,” but elsewhere (and in reviewer-facing bullets) implies a **three-layer framework** (byte budget, MAC efficiency, TDMA airtime). The text now says \(C_{\text{raw}}=C_{\text{coord,info}}/\gamma\) is “unit conversion, not a third test,” which is directionally right—but then \(\gamma\) is *not* merely conversion; it is a **model of per-slot time overhead** that changes schedulability. This needs a crisp statement: \(\gamma\) is not an independent feasibility layer, but it is a **parameter linking Test A’s bytes to Test B’s time**.
* The **ARQ demand vs. margin** reasoning mixes (i) expected retransmissions and (ii) reserved retransmission slots. The algorithm uses \(T_{\text{ARQ}}=M_r T_{\text{slot}}\) (reserved worst-case), while the narrative cites “GE demand \(\sim 726\) ms” (expected). Those are different design philosophies; the paper should pick one per claim or present both explicitly.

## 4. Clarity & Structure  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
Overall organization is strong for a sizing paper: definitions early, feasibility tests boxed, rate ladder table, then sensitivity. The repeated reminders about what is and is not used for recommendations (Model C vs S) are helpful.

Areas to improve:
* The manuscript is long and sometimes repetitive (multiple restatements of “24 kbps infeasible; 35 kbps recommended”). Consider consolidating into one “Design Outcome” subsection and referencing it.
* Some quantities are introduced in notation but behave as “derived schedule outputs” (e.g., \(\alpha_{\text{RX}}\)); this is fine, but readers may miss that it is **rate-dependent** and not a free knob.

Remaining concerns:

# Major Issues

1) **Campaign duty factor \(d\): realism improved, but still under-justified and under-coupled to operations**  
**Why it matters:** \(d\) is central to defusing the “46% overhead” concern. You now state that the stress case is episodic (<1%), which is good, but the mapping from mission phases to \(d\) is still largely asserted. Also, campaign correlation models (Bernoulli vs ON/OFF) materially change tails/buffer sizing.  
**Remedy:**  
  * Provide a short, quantitative derivation for at least one phase (e.g., orbit-raising): number of maneuver updates per orbit/day → commands per cycle → implied \(p_{\text{cmd}}\) and \(d\).  
  * Separate “**command generation probability within campaign**” \(p_{\text{cmd}}\) from “**fraction of time in campaign**” \(d\) more rigorously (a short stochastic model: alternating renewal ON/OFF with mean ON duration \(L_{\text{on}}\), mean OFF set by \(d\)).  
  * Add a sensitivity plot/table showing \(\eta\) and buffer factor \(M\) vs. \((d, L_{\text{on}})\) to demonstrate robustness of the “routine 5–10%” claim.

2) **\(\gamma\) unification (0.76 vs earlier 0.85): mostly consistent, but still risks “silent mixing” across sections**  
**Why it matters:** The PHY recommendation hinges on \(\gamma(R_{\text{PHY}})\). Any residual use of older or simplified \(\gamma\) values undermines trust. Version CX does a good job labeling Model C vs S, but there are still places where readers can misapply numbers (e.g., using \(\gamma_{24}\) in Eq. (17) while discussing 30 kbps feasibility).  
**Remedy:**  
  * Add a single “**authoritative \(\gamma\) table**” early (or in the boxed feasibility section) listing \(\gamma_{24},\gamma_{30},\gamma_{35},\gamma_{50}\) and explicitly stating: “All decision tables use these; Model S only for comparison.”  
  * In equations like \(C_{\text{TDMA}}=(k_c-1)S8/(T_c\gamma)\), explicitly annotate “\(\gamma=\gamma(R_{\text{PHY}})\)” right next to the equation to prevent readers from treating \(\gamma\) as constant.

3) **Three-layer feasibility framing remains conceptually muddy (bytes vs \(\gamma\) vs airtime)**  
**Why it matters:** Practitioners will copy the sizing method. Confusion about whether “MAC efficiency” is a separate test leads to double-counting or incorrect margins—exactly the failure mode you warn about.  
**Remedy:** Reframe clearly as:  
  * **Layer 1:** Information-byte budget (\(\eta_{\text{total}}\le 1\)).  
  * **Layer 2:** Time budget with explicit slot timing (Test B).  
  * \(\gamma\) is **not a layer**, but a *parameterization* of slot timing that maps bytes to time.  
Then remove (or strictly demote) any language that implies “MAC efficiency” is independently checkable beyond Test B.

4) **ARQ feasibility claims conflate reserved-slot design with stochastic demand**  
**Why it matters:** “ARQ infeasible at 30 kbps but feasible at 35 kbps” is a key conclusion. It must be clear whether you mean: (a) worst-case reserved retransmission window, (b) expected retransmissions under GE, or (c) deadline-miss probability under a specific ARQ scheduling policy. Currently, the text uses all three interchangeably.  
**Remedy:**  
  * Define the ARQ policy precisely: fixed \(M_r\) reserved slots per cycle? per-node or pooled? what happens if unused?  
  * Report ARQ feasibility in one consistent metric: e.g., “deadline miss probability under policy X with parameters Y.”  
  * If you want to keep the “demand \(\sim\) 726 ms” statement, label it as **expected additional airtime**, and contrast with the **reserved window** approach used in Algorithm 1.

5) **DES “verification value” is still limited; distributional tail result depends on an arbitrary campaign model**  
**Why it matters:** You correctly state DES matching analytic means is not validation. The remaining “non-tautological” value is the buffer CDF under burstiness, but that is only as credible as the burst model.  
**Remedy:**  
  * Provide at least one additional burst model (e.g., heavy-tailed ON durations or self-similar traffic) to show whether the “1.3× mean buffer” rule is stable or brittle.  
  * Alternatively, explicitly scope the buffer rule as “for geometric ON/OFF only” and move it to limitations, reducing its prominence.

6) **Packet-level validation in Section IV-J is not independent validation (and the manuscript admits this), but it is still used rhetorically to “anchor” recommendations**  
**Why it matters:** In top-tier venues, “anchoring” via standards framing is useful, but it should not be presented as empirical validation. You mostly do this correctly; however, the paper still leans on DVB-RCS2 measured \(\gamma\) to justify robustness in a way that may overreach (RCS2 is not ISL, different dynamics).  
**Remedy:**  
  * Tighten language: call it “**parameter plausibility bound**,” not validation.  
  * Add a short discussion of what aspects plausibly transfer (preamble/guard/FEC overhead structure) and what does not (propagation, Doppler, acquisition dynamics, terminal class).

7) **Generalized \(\gamma\) expression: useful, but missing a practitioner-ready “measurement recipe” and error bars**  
**Why it matters:** Eq. (29) is one of the most reusable outputs. Practitioners need to know how to measure each term and what uncertainty dominates.  
**Remedy:**  
  * Provide a short step-by-step recipe: how to measure \(T_{\text{acq}}\), turnaround, guard contributors, effective \(R_{\text{FEC}}\), and whether framing is FEC-coded.  
  * Add an uncertainty propagation example (even simple): if \(T_{\text{acq}}=5\pm2\) ms and \(T_{\text{guard}}=4.7\pm1\) ms, what is \(\gamma\) range and resulting \(R_{\text{PHY,min}}\) range?

# Minor Issues

1) **Baseline vs overhead notation:** sometimes \(\eta\) is “beyond baseline,” sometimes “protocol overhead” including heartbeats/summaries; you define it, but consider a consistent phrase like “\(\eta\) (non-baseline utilization).”

2) **Eq. (22) / stagger equations:** clarify whether \(T_{\text{cmd}}\) includes acquisition/guard/framing consistent with Model C; it appears yes, but should be explicit.

3) **Table 3 (Parameter Sensitivity Summary):** “AoI P99 = 440 s regardless of \(C_{\text{node}}\)” is only true under sampling-limited exception policy; add a footnote that it assumes no deadline misses/drops.

4) **Fleet reuse (Eq. 18):** the “non-binding at \(f_{\text{RF}}\le 1.2\%\)” statement depends heavily on \(R=3\) and \(F=4\). Provide one sentence showing the arithmetic for that threshold.

5) **Guard interval accounting:** the ACK mini-slot “inside jitter sub-slot” is clever but nonstandard; add a diagram or explicit timing offset description to avoid skepticism.

6) **Link budget claims:** “max >200 kbps per cluster” is asserted; include the key assumptions (bandwidth, modulation, required \(E_b/N_0\), implementation losses) in a compact table or appendix.

7) **Citation hygiene:** multiple “non-archival accessed Feb 2026” references; acceptable, but try to replace at least some with archival sources where possible.

## Overall Recommendation  
**Recommendation:** **Major Revision**

The paper has a solid core contribution: a clear, equation-driven sizing workflow that links message models to TDMA airtime feasibility, with a useful standards-based \(\gamma(R)\) parameterization and a defensible “35 kbps recommended” outcome under stated assumptions. Version CX substantially improves transparency: it better contextualizes the 46% stress case as a continuous-duty upper bound, clarifies that GE is a what-if tool, and is more explicit about Model C being the basis for recommendations.

The main barriers to acceptance are (i) lingering conceptual ambiguity about the “layering” of feasibility and the role of \(\gamma\), (ii) ARQ feasibility claims that mix expected-demand and reserved-window logic, and (iii) limited evidentiary value of DES/packet-level components beyond confirming the same underlying equations. These are fixable with tighter definitions, a more rigorous duty-factor derivation, and clearer separation of conditional design guidance vs. validation.

---

## Constructive Suggestions (ordered by impact)

1) **Unify feasibility narrative:** Present the method as exactly two tests, with \(\gamma\) as the mapping parameter; remove any residual “third layer” ambiguity.

2) **Make ARQ analysis internally consistent:** Choose (and clearly state) whether ARQ provisioning is worst-case reserved slots or probabilistic; report feasibility accordingly (deadline miss probability under a defined policy).

3) **Strengthen \(d\) realism:** Provide at least one quantitative operational derivation of \(d\) and show sensitivity over \((d, L_{\text{on}})\) to support the “routine 5–10%” claim.

4) **Improve practitioner usability of Eq. (29):** Add a measurement recipe + uncertainty example so \(\gamma\) can be instantiated from bench tests.

5) **Reduce repetition and tighten scope:** Consolidate repeated rate recommendations; demote Model S content further (or move to appendix) to avoid readers treating it as an alternative baseline.

6) **Increase robustness of DES tail claims:** Either add an additional burst model or clearly limit the buffer-sizing heuristic to the assumed ON/OFF process.

7) **Clarify what “packet-level validation” is:** Rename it to “standards-based parameterization” throughout and avoid any implication of empirical validation transfer from DVB-RCS2 to ISL.

If these revisions are made, the paper would be much closer to the rigor and clarity expected in IEEE TAES while retaining its strong system-engineering utility.
