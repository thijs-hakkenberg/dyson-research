# Peer Review of Manuscript Version AM

## 1. Significance & Novelty  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
The manuscript addresses an important and under-quantified question in space economics: when large-scale space infrastructure should transition from Earth-launched manufacturing to ISRU-based manufacturing. The integration of NPV timing, learning curves, stochastic capital uncertainty, dynamic “vitamin” fractions, and re-crossing analysis is novel in combination. The paper is potentially valuable for *Advances in Space Research*, especially as a decision-support framework rather than a definitive forecast. Its novelty lies less in any individual method than in assembling these methods into a coherent ISRU-vs-Earth crossover model.

## 2. Methodological Soundness  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The modeling framework is ambitious and substantially improved: the Monte Carlo treatment, correlated sampling, Kaplan–Meier censoring treatment, phased capital, dynamic vitamin fraction, and sensitivity sweeps are all appropriate directions. However, several methodological issues remain serious: the NPV comparison does not fully control for unequal service timing; the re-crossing/permanence definitions mix asymptotic per-unit logic with discounted cumulative NPV logic; some equations use inconsistent capital treatment; and several numerical statements appear inconsistent with the stated learning-curve equations. These issues are fixable, but they currently weaken confidence in the headline probabilities.

## 3. Validity & Logic  
**Rating: 2 (Below Average)**  
The paper’s central qualitative conclusion may be plausible, but several internal inconsistencies must be resolved before the quantitative claims can be accepted. Most importantly, the abstract and conclusion state that 95% of draws place a 20,000-unit program within the ISRU savings window, while Table 4/Table `savings_survival` appears to compute this conditional on converging runs only. Unconditionally, the probability cannot exceed the 85.1% convergence rate and appears closer to 80.6% at 20,000 units. There are also inconsistencies in asymptotic floor calculations, launch-learning numerical examples, discounting interpretation, and the treatment of censored re-crossing volumes.

## 4. Clarity & Structure  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The manuscript is much clearer than a typical early-stage model paper and contains many helpful tables. The canonical configuration table is especially useful. However, the paper is now very long and contains several overlapping baselines: deterministic $K=50$B, MC median $K=65$B, lump-sum capital, phased capital, pure Wright, plateau Wright, dynamic vitamins, and logistic saturation. The result is that a reader can lose track of which configuration supports which claim. The configuration-to-crossover table helps, but additional pruning and stricter labeling of conditional/unconditional quantities are needed.

## 5. Ethical Compliance  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
The AI-use disclosure is unusually explicit and appropriate. The statement that numerical outputs were generated by deterministic/stochastic code rather than accepted directly from AI tools is important and welcome. Code availability is promised, but the repository commit is still listed as `PENDING`; for publication, the authors should archive a frozen release with DOI, requirements file, seed, test suite, and reproduction instructions. Until then, reproducibility is asserted but not fully demonstrated.

## 6. Scope & Referencing  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The manuscript covers much of the relevant ISRU, learning-curve, launch-cost, and space-economics literature. The use of Wertz, NASA CEH, Jones, Sanders, Sowers, O’Neill, and learning-curve literature is appropriate. Additional references would strengthen the work in three areas: aerospace cost-estimating practice for high-volume spacecraft production, lunar/cislunar transport cost models, and technology disruption/real-options treatment. Starship-class cost assumptions should be clearly marked as projections rather than empirical launch prices.

## 7. Economic / NPV Decision Framing  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate, but needs major revision)**  
The inclusion of phased capital, revenue breakeven, hybrid transition, and technical success probability is a major strength. However, the cost-only NPV comparison rewards delayed expenditure: because ISRU delivers later, its operating costs are discounted more heavily, while Earth costs are incurred earlier and therefore have higher present value. This is not a service-equivalent comparison unless early Earth delivery has zero value and Earth production cannot be optimally delayed. The revenue-delay section partially addresses this, but a common-delivery or deadline-constrained comparison is needed to make the NPV result decision-equivalent.

## 8. Monte Carlo & Statistical Robustness  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate to Good)**  
The Monte Carlo framework is strong in concept: 10,000 runs, copula correlations, PRCC, bootstrap intervals, KM treatment, and $K$-median sweeps are all useful. The authors have improved the treatment of censoring. However, conditional vs. unconditional metrics are not consistently distinguished; the savings-window result is likely misreported; the count of stochastic parameters appears inconsistent; and censored $N^{**}$ values are reported too definitively. The paper should report unconditional probabilities alongside conditional statistics throughout.

## 9. Aerospace / ISRU Engineering Realism  
**Rating: 3 (Adequate)**  
The model is useful as a parametric economics framework, but the ISRU engineering basis remains weak relative to the Earth pathway. The paper acknowledges this, which is good. The $K$-median sweep is one of the strongest additions because it exposes the dependence on poorly known capital cost. Still, the canonical model should either include recurring maintenance/spares/replacement costs or justify their exclusion more strongly, since the appendix shows maintenance can shift crossover by thousands of units. The vitamin BOM is clearer than before, but still needs reconciliation between “15% Earth-sourced” and “5% modeled.”

## 10. Practical Decision Value  
**Rating: 4 (Good)**  
The manuscript has practical value because it provides decision-relevant quantities: crossover probability, KM median, savings window, $K$ sensitivity, success-probability threshold, revenue breakeven, and hybrid option value. The decision tree figure is potentially useful, but it should include uncertainty ranges or be explicitly labeled as illustrative. The technology disruption section is improved but still too deterministic to support strong claims about obsolescence resilience.

# Major Issues

## 1. Conditional vs. unconditional savings-window probability is misreported  
**Issue:** The abstract and conclusion state that “95% of draws” place a 20,000-unit program within the ISRU savings window. But Table `savings_survival` says the probability is computed over converging runs, while only 85.1% of all runs converge at $r=5\%$. Therefore the unconditional probability cannot be 94.7%.  

**Why it matters:** This is a headline result. Reporting a conditional probability as if it applies to all parameter draws materially overstates robustness.  

**Remedy:** Report both:  
- Conditional savings-window probability among converged runs: about 94.7%.  
- Unconditional probability over all MC draws: approximately $0.851 \times 0.947 \approx 80.6\%$, apparently matching Table `convergence` at 20,000 units.  
Revise the abstract, conclusion, Table `savings_survival`, and all “95% of draws” language accordingly.

## 2. NPV comparison is not service-equivalent because ISRU delivers later  
**Issue:** The pathway-specific NPV formulation discounts Earth costs earlier and ISRU costs later. This makes delayed ISRU expenditures look cheaper, even before accounting for the value of earlier Earth delivery.  

**Why it matters:** A cost-only NPV comparison without common service timing can bias the result toward the slower pathway. Even non-revenue infrastructure has schedule value, mission utility, or deadline constraints.  

**Remedy:** Add at least one common-timing sensitivity:  
1. Earth production optimally delayed to match the ISRU delivery schedule.  
2. Both pathways constrained to deliver by a common deadline.  
3. A shadow service-value term, of which the revenue analysis is a special case.  
Then clarify which result is the primary decision metric.

## 3. Re-crossing and permanence analysis is improved but still not fully adequate  
**Issue:** The $N^{**}$ analysis is a substantial improvement, but the formalism mixes asymptotic per-unit permanence with discounted cumulative NPV permanence. Under positive discounting, an asymptotic per-unit disadvantage does not necessarily imply finite re-crossing because future differences are exponentially attenuated. Also, Eq. `recrossing` uses $+K$ rather than the same phased discounted $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$ used in Eq. `crossover_npv`.  

**Why it matters:** The paper’s claim that most transient crossovers are “functionally permanent” depends on this analysis.  

**Remedy:** Define three distinct concepts explicitly:  
- Undiscounted asymptotic per-unit dominance.  
- Discounted cumulative NPV re-crossing.  
- Finite-horizon savings-window survival.  
Use the same discounted capital treatment in $N^*$ and $N^{**}$. Report censored $N^{**}$ as lower bounds, e.g., “median $N^{**} > 200{,}000$,” not “median $N^{**}=200{,}000$.”

## 4. Asymptotic cost-floor thresholds appear inconsistent  
**Issue:** The manuscript states a permanent crossover threshold such as $C_{\mathrm{floor}} < m p_{\mathrm{launch}} - m p_{\mathrm{transport}}\alpha = \$1.67$M. But in the canonical launch-learning model, the Earth asymptotic cost is $C_{\mathrm{mat}} + m p_{\mathrm{fuel}}$, not $m p_{\mathrm{launch}}$. With baseline values this gives roughly $1.0$M + $0.37$M, before vitamin terms.  

**Why it matters:** The permanence/transience classification and “failure mode” thresholds depend directly on these asymptotic expressions.  

**Remedy:** Re-derive all asymptotic thresholds under each active configuration: constant launch, launch-learning, dynamic vitamin, and plateau. Present one canonical derivation using the exact equations used in the MC code.

## 5. Launch-learning numerical examples appear inconsistent with the stated Wright curve  
**Issue:** The text says that at baseline scale the ops component under $\mathrm{LR}_L=0.97$ declines by less than 0.3%. But with $b_L=\ln(0.97)/\ln 2$, at $n\sim 4{,}400$ the factor is approximately $0.69$, i.e., a roughly 31% reduction in the learnable component. Similarly, at $n=10{,}000$ and $\mathrm{LR}_L=0.90$, the learnable component should be about $800 \times 0.90^{\log_2(10000)} \approx \$200$/kg, not $\sim\$334$/kg.  

**Why it matters:** These inconsistencies raise concern about whether the text, tables, and code are aligned.  

**Remedy:** Audit all learning-curve calculations, regenerate the launch-learning sweep, and add a unit test verifying several analytical values of $n^b$ for stated learning rates.

## 6. Earth learning offset $n_0$ sensitivity is useful but under-motivated  
**Issue:** The appendix includes an $n_0 \times \mathrm{LR}_E$ table, but $n_0$ is not clearly defined in the model equations or motivated by product heritage. Since $\mathrm{LR}_E$ is one of the two dominant drivers, this sensitivity deserves stronger treatment.  

**Why it matters:** If Earth manufacturing starts with substantial heritage, Earth costs may be lower than assumed, delaying or preventing crossover in fast-learning cases.  

**Remedy:** Define $n_0$ formally, e.g., replacing $n$ with $n+n_0$ in the Earth learning curve. Justify plausible $n_0$ values for truss segments, panels, and habitat shells. Consider including $n_0$ as a stochastic parameter or moving the sensitivity to the main text.

## 7. Vitamin BOM is clearer, but the modeled fraction remains ambiguous  
**Issue:** Table `vitamin_bom` lists 15% Earth-sourced mass, but the cost model uses only 5% as $f_v$. It also says sensors/wiring are not included in $f_v$ because they are accounted for in integration overhead, but no explicit integration-overhead cost term is visible in the ISRU pathway.  

**Why it matters:** Vitamin mass and cost strongly affect permanence and can eliminate crossover at high $c_{\mathrm{vit}}$.  

**Remedy:** Either:  
- Model the initial vitamin fraction as 15% decaying to a lower floor; or  
- Relabel the table to distinguish “Earth-sourced at early maturity” from “irreducible modeled vitamin fraction.”  
State exactly where coatings, seals, sensors, and wiring costs enter the model.

## 8. Technology obsolescence treatment remains too thin  
**Issue:** The manuscript now includes deterministic disruption scenarios, which is an improvement. However, technology obsolescence over a 20–30 year horizon is broader than two step changes at fixed $n=2{,}000$.  

**Why it matters:** The ISRU pathway requires large upfront capital and long deployment times, making it vulnerable to Earth manufacturing automation, launch-system breakthroughs, in-space assembly advances, or ISRU facility obsolescence.  

**Remedy:** Add a small stochastic technology-disruption model or a structured scenario matrix varying disruption timing, magnitude, and affected pathway. Alternatively, explicitly state that obsolescence is only qualitatively bounded and not fully modeled.

## 9. $K$ grounding and recurring maintenance need stronger integration  
**Issue:** The $K$-median sweep is excellent and should remain central. However, the canonical MC excludes recurring maintenance, while the appendix shows maintenance can shift crossover by +1,435 to +5,515 units. For a lunar industrial facility, recurring spares, refurbishment, and replacement are not optional edge cases.  

**Why it matters:** If maintenance is material, the canonical crossover probability is optimistic.  

**Remedy:** Include a maintenance/spares term in the canonical MC, even if broadly distributed, or justify why it is already embedded in $C_{\mathrm{floor}}$ or $K$. If embedded, avoid double-counting in the appendix.

## 10. Decision tree is useful but should show uncertainty  
**Issue:** The decision tree adds practical value, but current thresholds appear deterministic or point-estimate based.  

**Why it matters:** The paper’s main contribution is probabilistic. A deterministic decision tree can imply false precision.  

**Remedy:** Add threshold ranges or annotations: e.g., $N^*$ conditional median vs. KM median, $K$-dependent crossover probability, $p_s^{\min}$ horizon dependence, and revenue breakeven range. If space is limited, move the current tree to the appendix and provide a simplified probabilistic decision chart in the main text.

# Minor Issues

1. Replace remaining “validate” language such as “validate the PRCC rankings” with “support” or “are consistent with.” The “validated” language is mostly softened, but a few residual phrases remain too strong.

2. Correct the stochastic-parameter count. Table `params` appears to contain 19 independent stochastic parameters plus 2 derived, not 18 plus 2.

3. Correct $\sigma_{\ln}$ descriptions. For an unclipped lognormal, P90/P50 is $\exp(1.2816\sigma)$; for $\sigma=1.0$ this is about 3.6, not 4.1. With clipping, Table `sigma_ln` gives a different value. State clearly whether ratios are theoretical or clipped.

4. The appendix text on convergence as a function of horizon appears inconsistent with the main Table `convergence` values. Align these numbers.

5. The PRCC interpretation for $t_0$ is inconsistent: the table gives negative PRCC but says “later ramp-up delays.” If the output is volume $N^*$, negative PRCC means higher $t_0$ lowers the crossover volume, likely because costs are discounted later. Clarify volume vs. calendar-time effects.

6. Eq. `logistic_saturation` appears conceptually problematic: dividing the Wright term by $1+(n/n_{\mathrm{half}})^2$ accelerates convergence to a floor rather than representing slowed learning. Also, $C_{\mathrm{mat}}=m p_{\mathrm{fuel}}$ conflates manufacturing material cost with launch fuel floor.

7. The production schedule equation gives negative cumulative production for $t<t_0$ unless piecewise corrected. This is acceptable if never evaluated there, but the text should make the domain explicit.

8. Avoid exact “100.0%” claims from finite MC runs. Use “no re-crossings observed within 100,000 units” or provide binomial confidence bounds.

9. Clarify whether $p_{\mathrm{transport}}$ includes tug capital amortization, propellant, operations, and return/reuse assumptions.

10. The statement that Earth cumulative cost is dominated by launch cost is not consistent with the baseline numbers at crossover, where Earth manufacturing cost still appears larger than launch cost. Reframe the driver as Earth recurring manufacturing plus launch floor, with LR$_E$ as the dominant non-capital uncertainty.

11. Use consistent notation for $C_{\mathrm{ops}}$, $C_{\mathrm{ops}}^{\mathrm{vit}}$, and yield-adjusted costs in the NPV equations.

12. The code availability statement should provide a frozen commit, release tag, DOI archive, and exact reproduction command before publication.

# Overall Recommendation  
**Recommendation: Major Revision**

This is a promising and unusually comprehensive manuscript. Its strengths are the integrated cost model, explicit uncertainty propagation, strong sensitivity treatment of $K$, inclusion of dynamic vitamin fractions, recognition of transient vs. permanent crossovers, and the addition of revenue-delay and technical-success probability analyses. The paper has clear potential to contribute to the space economics and ISRU literature as a transparent decision-support model.

However, several headline quantitative claims are not yet reliable as written. The most serious issue is the apparent confusion between conditional and unconditional savings-window probabilities. The re-crossing analysis is valuable but needs more rigorous censoring treatment and consistency with the NPV formulation. The NPV comparison also requires a service-equivalent timing sensitivity, because the current formulation partly rewards ISRU for delivering later. Finally, several numerical examples, especially launch-learning calculations and asymptotic floor thresholds, should be audited against the equations and code.

If these issues are corrected, the manuscript would become substantially stronger. I would not reject it because the core framework is useful and the authors have clearly made serious improvements. But the paper requires major revision before its headline claims can be accepted.

# Constructive Suggestions, Ordered by Impact

1. **Recompute and relabel all savings-window probabilities as conditional and unconditional.** Make the unconditional value the primary abstract/conclusion claim.

2. **Add a common-delivery or deadline-constrained NPV sensitivity.** This will address the central concern that pathway-specific timing biases cost-only NPV comparisons.

3. **Reformulate the permanence/re-crossing section.** Use consistent discounted capital, report censored $N^{**}$ properly, and distinguish asymptotic per-unit permanence from discounted cumulative permanence.

4. **Audit the learning-curve implementation and numerical examples.** Add simple analytical unit tests for LR$_E$, LR$_I$, and LR$_L$.

5. **Strengthen the $n_0$ and Earth-learning discussion.** Since LR$_E$ is dominant, production heritage should be explicitly modeled or better justified.

6. **Clarify the vitamin BOM and dynamic fraction.** Reconcile 15% total Earth-sourced content with 5% modeled vitamin mass.

7. **Move maintenance/spares into the canonical MC or justify exclusion.** The appendix shows this can be a major driver.

8. **Expand the technology obsolescence treatment.** Either add stochastic disruption timing/magnitude or explicitly downgrade claims about robustness to disruption.

9. **Simplify the presentation of baselines.** Keep the canonical configuration central and move secondary variants to the appendix unless they affect headline claims.

10. **Archive the code and reproduction environment before resubmission.** This is especially important given the number of interacting assumptions and sensitivity cases.
